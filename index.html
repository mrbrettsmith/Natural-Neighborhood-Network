About The Project

Even the smallest garden helps to create a healthier, and more beautiful, community. Sustainable plantings look different than a normal lawn, and our signage describes what the garden is and the positive impact it has on the area.

Some projects focus on a single issue while others expand to address many, and the signage grows to cover them all. When connected with other Natural Neighborhood Network gardens we can see the power of our community working together, and inspire others to start their own sustainable projects.

Rain Garden

Rain Gardens are shallow bowl shaped gardens that create a natural place for stormwater to collect. They keep rain from running off into the street where it collects pollutants before flowing into storm drains that feed local lakes and waterways. In addition to keeping the immediate area clean they help protect bodies of water far outside the city.

The wet sandy soil allows plants to thrive, creating a lush habitat for pollinators and beneficial animal life. Rain Gardens are wonderful for deep-rooted native plants and flowers, and always add an interesting feature to the neighborhood.

Prairie Restoration

A prairie is home to grasses, herbs, and flowers. In Minnesota, prairie grassland is one of the four biomes that make our region unique. The tall grasses and colorful wildflowers provide dramatic visual interest in every season, and provide an attractive source of food and shelter for grassland birds. Prairie plants have extremely deep and strong root systems that prevent erosion and filter contaminants. Native grasses offer texture to a garden, and native wildflowers can provide a rainbow of colors.

Pollinator gardens are an oasis for bees and butterflies in urban areas. They form a pathway for pollinators to migrate, supporting biodiversity and helping plants propagate. Native wildflowers become the backdrop to a bustling show of butterflies, bees, and birds. Pollinator gardens are a fantastic way to add life and activity to the neighborhood.

Urban Forest

The tree canopy cools cities and provides protection from severe weather events. The shade from a tree helps keep concrete and asphalt from absorbing the heat of the sun, causing a dramatic reduction in urban temperatures. Trees also keep homes cool in the summer, and allow light in during the winter months. Many underserved areas of our community lack sufficient tree coverage, and Urban Forests help solve that problem. Planting a variety of tree species helps build a diverse woodland that is resistant to invasive pests and disease

Free Seed Library

A seed library helps your neighbors start their own sustainable gardening projects. By offering free seeds you are inviting others to learn about the benefits of gardening while supporting projects that will amplify the impact of your sustainable project. Not only will new sustainable gardens make your neighborhood become more attractive, their beneficial properties will link to others in the community creating more habitats for native plants and animals. A free seed library can be as simple as a container on a fence or as complicated as lawn sculpture, but it will be an invitation to a healthy neighborhood.
